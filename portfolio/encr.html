Timeline of Encryption




Circa 60 BC: Julius Caesar invents a cypher that shifts characters by a few places down in the alphabet. For example, a to b, b to c, and so on. It's a very simple shift yet still took the people at the time a long time to decypher it.



1553: Giovan Battista Bellaso envisions encryption, but instead of simply shifting letters a few positions, there's an agreed upon key that the recipient knows to decode the message



1854: Charles Wheatstone invents the Playfair Cipher, which encrypts pairs of letters instead of single ones. This is similar to Bellaso's vision and like the one we talked about in class with keys



1917: Edward Hebern invents the electro-mechanical machine. The key is in a rotating disc and is the first example of a rotor machine. It encodes a substitution table that is changed every time a new character is typed and is similar to the programming of Enigma



1918: Arthur Scherbius invents the Enigma machine, but it uses several rotors instead of Hebern's one. Throughout WWII, Germans used Enigma to send encrypted messages



1932: Marian Rejewski discovered how Enigma works. This allows mathematicians and cryptographers such as Alan Turing to decode Enigma in 1940 which lead to Turing's Bombe Machine



1945: Claude E. Shannon of Bell Labs published an article called "A mathematical theory of cryptography." It's the starting point of modern cryptography.








This is only a small glimpse into the history of cryptography. To learn more, visit this website
